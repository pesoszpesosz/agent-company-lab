## What Agents Can And Cannot Do

Agents can:

- scan public sources;
- normalize opportunities;
- compare payouts, deadlines, rules, and account gates;
- draft local proof artifacts;
- build prototypes and benchmark baselines;
- prepare service-request packets for the user or service workers.

Agents must not:

- perform paid human-only tasks;
- create accounts or accept terms;
- submit marketplace listings, studies, bug reports, proposals, PRs, or competition entries;
- post affiliate links or outreach;
- pretend to be the user;
- trade, deposit, withdraw, connect wallets, or provide payment details.

### 1. `money_source_discovery`

This is the recurring seeker layer the user described: an agent that constantly looks for new parts that need to be tested.

Why first:

- It is fully agent-suited.
- It reduces stale memory.
- It gives the CEO a weekly delta report instead of random chat discoveries.
- It can feed every other lane.

First proof task:

Create a no-side-effect weekly source sweep format with fields:

- source URL
- money path

- payout/prize/commission/pay claim
- account/KYC/payment gate
- public-action gate
- expected value band
- competition/crowding risk
- recommended owner lane

### 2. `ai_ml_competitions`

Source signals:

- Kaggle competitions currently show active money-prize competitions, including large AI prize listings such as ARC Prize 2026 search results.
- DrivenData says its competitions have provided more than $4.9M in prizes and drawn more than 260,000 submissions.
- ML Contests aggregates data science, ML, and robotics competitions.
- lablab.ai has ongoing AI hackathon listings in 2026.
- ETHGlobal remains a major web3 hackathon surface.

Agent fit:

High. Agents can run local baselines, write notebooks, compare rules, search public kernels/papers, generate experiment plans, and produce reproducible reports.

Gates:

- account registration
- team eligibility
- competition rules
- data license
- compute/API cost
- final submission

First proof task:

Scout five active competitions and score them by prize, deadline, dataset availability, baseline feasibility, compute cost, and agent-assisted iteration potential. Do not submit.

### 3. `digital_products_templates_plugins`

Source signals:

- Gumroad positions itself as a route to "first dollar" digital product sales.
- Lemon Squeezy sells digital products, software subscriptions, and SaaS billing globally.
- PromptBase now explicitly advertises selling prompts and agent `skill.md` files.
- Notion Marketplace lets creators submit templates and potentially get paid.
- Shopify's app/theme ecosystem offers app and theme revenue routes, with App Store registration and revenue-share requirements.

Agent fit:

High for research and creation; medium for distribution. Agents can build templates, prompt packs, skill packs, plugin prototypes, documentation, demos, screenshots, and launch packets. Distribution requires user approval, account/payment/tax setup, and public listing review.

First proof task:

Produce three local prototype packets:

- one Codex skill/template pack;
- one Notion/ops dashboard template;
- one automation/script microproduct.

Each packet needs buyer pain, artifact path, screenshots or docs, price hypothesis, marketplace route, and gate checklist.

### 4. `productized_services_marketplaces`

Source signals:

- Upwork describes itself as a large freelance marketplace and emphasizes AI-amplified experts.
- Fiverr has categories for AI, app development, data tagging/annotation, web, and tech services.
- Shopify Partners can route commerce services, apps, and themes.

Agent fit:

Medium-high if the company sells narrow deliverables backed by proof artifacts:

- website intake audit;
- AI automation setup packet;
- Shopify app/theme diagnostic;
- security hardening memo;
- QA bug report pack;
- analytics dashboard setup.

Gates:

No account setup, proposal, email, DM, contact form, or marketplace message without the outreach and public-action service gates.

First proof task:

Create three fictional sample service packets with scope, fixed deliverables, turnaround time, proof artifact, target customer filter, and anti-spam rule. Do not identify or contact real leads.

### 5. `qa_usability_testing_gigs`

Source signals:

- uTest describes itself as a large digital freelance software tester community.
- UserTesting has a "get paid to test" participant route.
- Test IO invites freelance testers to work remotely and earn extra money testing apps.
- Applause describes uTest members using personal devices to test mobile and web applications.

Agent fit:

Research/preparation only. Paid crowdtesting and user testing expect real human testers and real devices. Agents can prepare:

- eligibility packet;
- device inventory;
- bug-report templates;
- practice defects from local demo sites;
- payment/account/KYC checklist.

First proof task:

Create a QA practice kit with one sample defect report, one usability feedback template, and one account-gate comparison table.

### 6. `ai_training_eval_gigs`

Source signals:

- DataAnnotation advertises remote AI-training work and expert tasks.
- Outlier advertises freelance AI trainer opportunities and public payout totals.
- Toloka provides training data for AI agents and LLMs.
- TELUS Digital AI advertises AI trainers/data annotators and related roles.
- Prolific has a participant route for paid studies and AI/human-data research.

Agent fit:

Research/preparation only. This is explicitly human work. Agents can:

- summarize eligibility requirements;
- compare pay claims and reliability risks;
- draft a user decision checklist;
- prepare skill-assessment study material;
- flag privacy/ethics concerns.

Agents must not do the work, submit answers, complete studies, or pretend to be the user.

First proof task:

Write a human-only eligibility and risk packet comparing DataAnnotation, Outlier, Toloka, TELUS Digital AI, and Prolific.

### 7. `affiliate_partner_programs`

Source signals:

- PartnerStack describes itself as a B2B SaaS partner network and has a partner-program marketplace.
- impact.com has an affiliate marketplace for creators/affiliates/publishers.
- Rewardful and FirstPromoter show examples of SaaS affiliate/referral infrastructure and recurring commission routes.

Agent fit:

Medium. Agents can scout programs and draft compliant content, but revenue depends on audience/distribution and honest endorsement.

Gates:

- affiliate account applications
- disclosure/compliance
- public content
- link placement
- outreach or SEO distribution

First proof task:

Create a partner-program scout table for 25 AI/SaaS tools with commission, approval requirements, cookie/term, content obligations, and fit to the user's existing X/profile/content assets. No applications or links.
